The question

How large are Chinese state subsidies, and where does the money actually go? Existing estimates come from the top down: the Kiel Institute's 2024 study put the figure at $2.37 trillion (roughly 15% of GDP), while CSIS estimated $231 billion in direct subsidies in 2019. These numbers are widely cited in trade policy debates, but they're macro estimates built from national accounts and firm-level disclosures.

Nobody has systematically looked at what local governments actually publish about their subsidy programs. This matters because in China's governance system, central policy sets direction while local governments write the checks. A State Council directive saying "support strategic emerging industries" becomes a Shenzhen district policy saying "500,000 yuan per registered AI algorithm."

This analysis uses the CNGOV corpus — {{ stats.total|default(0) }} government documents from {{ stats.site_count|default(0) }} Shenzhen sites — to map subsidy flows from the bottom up. We extracted specific yuan amounts, matched them to industry sectors, and traced which central policies generate which local subsidies.

What this tells us

  1. Bottom-up data reveals structure that top-down estimates miss. Macro estimates tell you the total; local documents tell you the mechanism. District-level subsidy policies show exactly which industries are targeted, what the eligibility criteria are, and how much money is available per firm. This is the level of detail that matters for competitive analysis and trade policy.
  2. Local subsidy publication is highly uneven. Some districts publish detailed subsidy measures with specific amounts; others publish nothing. This creates a "transparency gradient" where the most active districts are the most visible — and the silent ones may be spending just as much, or more, without public documentation.
  3. The central-to-local chain is traceable. Every district subsidy policy cites the higher-level documents that authorize it. This means you can build a complete audit trail from a State Council directive to a specific yuan amount in a specific district — the kind of evidence that trade lawyers and policy analysts need.
  4. Our numbers are a floor, not a ceiling. We can only extract amounts from documents with accessible body text (currently {{ subsidy_stats.documents_with_amounts }} documents). Many subsidy details are in PDF attachments, WeChat announcements, or unpublished internal documents. The true scale is certainly larger.
