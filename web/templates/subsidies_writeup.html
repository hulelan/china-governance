The question

How large are Chinese state subsidies, and where does the money actually go? Existing estimates come from the top down: the Kiel Institute's 2024 study put the figure at $2.37 trillion (roughly 15% of GDP), while CSIS estimated $231 billion in direct subsidies in 2019.

Nobody has systematically looked at what local governments actually publish about their subsidy programs. This analysis uses the ChinaGovernance corpus — {{ stats.total|default(0) }} government documents from {{ stats.site_count|default(0) }} sites — to map subsidy flows from the bottom up.

What this tells us

  1. Bottom-up data reveals structure that top-down estimates miss. District-level subsidy policies show exactly which industries are targeted and how much money is available per firm.
  2. Local subsidy publication is highly uneven. Some districts publish detailed measures; others publish nothing. This creates a "transparency gradient."
  3. The central-to-local chain is traceable. Every district subsidy policy cites the higher-level documents that authorize it — a complete audit trail.
  4. Our numbers are a floor, not a ceiling. Many subsidy details are in PDF attachments, WeChat announcements, or unpublished internal documents.
